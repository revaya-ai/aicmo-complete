# hook-library

## Purpose

Line one decides whether anyone reads line two. A hook is not a summary. It opens a gap the reader has to close. Pick one pattern, fill it with the real topic, keep it to five to ten words.

## The patterns

### Information gap
State that something is true and withhold the reason. The reader has to keep reading to get it.
- Example shape: "The product that sold out was the one we almost killed."

### Contrarian
Say the opposite of the common advice, then back it up in the body.
- Example shape: "A longer routine made my skin worse."

### Number
Lead with a specific, surprising count. Specific beats round.
- Example shape: "I tested 14 serums. Three were worth it."

### Question as statement
Ask a question the reader is already asking, in their words.
- Example shape: "Why does your skin look worse after a week off?"

### Callout
Name the exact reader and the exact moment.
- Example shape: "If you own a small brand and can't afford a CMO, read this."

### Confession
Admit a mistake. Honesty earns the next line.
- Example shape: "We shipped a product that did not work. Here is what it cost."

### Pattern interrupt
Break the expected frame in the first three words.
- Example shape: "Stop buying the serum."

## How to choose

1. Read the locked angle from the content-os Angle step.
2. Pick the pattern that creates the most real tension for this audience and pillar.
3. Fill it with the actual topic. No placeholder language.
4. Cut it to five to ten words. If it needs a comma to breathe, it is too long.

## Self-test

- Does it open a gap, or does it summarize? Summaries lose.
- Would the reader feel the need to read line two?
- Is it specific to this topic, or could it sit on any post?

## Rules

- Five to ten words.
- No em dashes or hyphens as breaks. No emojis.
- No throat-clearing. Never open with "in today's world".
